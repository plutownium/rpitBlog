The Cost of Music: About The Value of Hearing Quiet

By Roly Poly

The quiet of the forest is a gentle kind of bliss that you can experience in no other way.

The quiet of a late night suburban neighborhood speaks volumes about the interconnectedness we share merely by living amongst each other: The ambience of distant traffic whispers stories about life lived elsewhere, and the intense lull of activity the city faces at night time.

The quiet of a home library is a calm and solitary experience, one that is punctuated only by the crackle of burning logs and kindling, while smooth yet grainy paper is held beneath your thumb.

And I can’t perceive any of these quiet sounds anymore, because I damaged my hearing being a regular at, and working at, bars and nightclubs.

People don’t realize that the cost of hearing damage is that quiet sounds go away forever.

Here are two exercises that will show you what you’ll be missing. They will only work if you don’t already have hearing loss. If it’s too late, I wouldn’t do it: You’ll go away from it with regrets.

Exercise #1

As you’re going to bed, leave an inch wide opening in your window. Lay in bed. Listen to the neighborhood’s faintest sounds. Notice that you can hear distant sounds. What is the furthest sound you can hear? If you’re under 18, chances are you can hear stuff that happens a long ways away, because sound travels.

You’ll be able to hear cars passing by a full block away. The sounds of young people walking around at night. The quiet ambience of a hundred distant sounds blending together into a gentle hum. Though the lack of sound, the silence, implies non-movement, the ambient volume of those distant sound sources blending together will imply life: You will know that you are still around people, still connected to your neighborhood, to your city.

The nighttime sounds are a romance of sorts. A story that tells you that you are in the thick of a thousand stories you’ll never know. Each passing human sound will hint at someone living a life you are connected to only by the faintest logic, yet connected to nonetheless. Each rise and fall of the volume of the ambience will share a distant story like a secret whispered by a stranger.

This exercise will show you a form of beauty that you will wave goodbye to as your hearing degrades from exposure to loud music. The erosion of your hearing will happen over years, not weeks, and thus you’ll be unable to recognize the damage. You’ll tell yourself that it is okay to continue eroding your hearing until it is too late: Too bad, can’t hear the quiet anymore.

The point of the exercise is to recognize the beauty of the quiet, to stop taking the quiet for granted. For quiet is its own music.

Exercise #2

Odds are that you live in the city, because most people live in the city. But you can travel to a nearby spot of nature in less than two hours, so this exercise requires traveling to a nearby forest, desert, or mountain range. A lake would do nicely. So would an *empty* beach (a crowded one won’t work because of people talking) on a day when there are no waves.

This exercise demands sitting quietly in your chosen patch of forest or desert, on the top of a mountain (away from people), on the shore of a lake or the ocean. Whichever you choose, choose somewhere that is silent, or seems that way while your hearing is adjusted to the sound of you walking. Note that walking itself is quiet, and yet the threshold of your perception of sound will have to adjust for you to hear the truly quiet sounds of nature. You also need to choose a day when the wind is stagnant, because even gentle wind is louder than the most quiet of quiet sounds.

So, once you’re in your chosen spot of nature, find somewhere to sit and sit there for twenty minutes. The ideal location is near but not close to a river, so its movement is perceptible if you deliberately focus on the sound.

While you’re sitting, pay attention to the quietest of quiet sounds that you can barely hear against the backdrop of silence.

Can you hear a distant bird calling? The distant step of some animal? Perhaps you observe a loon dip into the lake a hundred feet away: Can you hear the distant “sploosh” as it enters the water? If you chose a mountain or a desert, what does the gentle movements of air tell you about the place you’re visiting?

Shift your feet on the ground. Can you hear the sound of your feet moving?

If there is a nearby river, can you hear the river? What do you know about the state of the river?

If there are distant birds, how quiet are the birds?

Final question: Do you experience beauty while doing this? Would you like to permanently stop yourself from experiencing the beauty?

What’s It Worth To You?

Recognize that damaging your hearing means saying goodbye to the above scenes of auditory beauty in a permanent way. The ringing in your ears caused by hearing damage is a mistake you cannot undo. There is no surgery, no pharmaceutical aid, that will undo the ringing. Once you gain the ringing in your ears, the songs silence sings will never be heard again. Instead, you will hear the ringing.

I don’t think going to a nightclub ten times a year is going to cause this issue, and indeed it would be fine if that was all it was: Ten times a year for a period of about five years, for a total of fifty visits. The issue is that even five visits a year, psychologically, puts you close to thirty visits a year, which is a waste of time all on its own. But worse, the thirty visits a year, or even ten visits a year, will add up to hearing damage.

Going to music events and nightlife establishments is short term fun. You have a good time for like four hours.

The cost is that, for the rest of your life, you have a little bit more difficulty perceiving quiet.

I remember thinking about the costs to my hearing, way back when. I did calculations every few months: “If I go out again, how much worse will my hearing be the next day?” The answer was always, “Too little to notice, so I’m going out.”

The issue is that the hearing loss happens gradually, too slow to be perceived. If you were able to A/B test the damage to your hearing, across two years, by flipping a switch, you would notice the damage. It would be obvious. “Oh, I can’t hear quiet as well as I used to.”

Young People Are The Dumbest Demographic, and Some Math

Young people consistently choose loud music venues, and repeat exposure to them, over a far higher probability that they will have great hearing, and no tinnitus, later on in life. People also make the twin decision, which is to pump the volume on their headphones or speaker system while at home, in the car, or at the gym. Both these activities guarantee some level of tinnitus.

Indeed, I’m not saying that the right amount of exposure to hearing damage is zero. I’ll say it again: Zero is the wrong amount! But as we go from 1 unit of exposure, to 2 units of exposure to 3 units of exposure, 1 unit of exposure yields K units of benefit. Does 2 units of exposure then yield K * 2 units of hedonistic goodness? No, it’s more like K * 1.98 units. And then 3 units of exposure doesn’t yield K * 3 units. It’s more like K * 1.94 units of benefit, K * 1.94 units of hedonistic goodness. I am saying that there is a decreasing marginal utility (this is an economics term) of exposure to loud music, meaning that as your exposure increases, the typical gain you get from exposure decreases disproportionately.

I also wanted to ask the reader to contemplate the likelihood that they’ll want to be able to perceive quiet later on in life. If the reader is a young person they might flippantly say that they’d prefer to “make the most” of being young and choose the nightclubs, music festival circuit and blaring headphones over being able to quietly, peacefully enjoy the silence of a library at age 63. Indeed, you might not even make it to 63! Doesn’t wisdom say to take advantage of youth while you have it?

No.

It does not.

Wisdom is recognizing that even if you don’t care about future you right now, future you will be stuck dealing with the consequences of younger you’s decisions for a long time.

Two hundred nights of fun (and sometimes the night out sucks!) spread out over eight years...

At a cost of 40 to 80 years of hearing an omnipresent “EeeeeeeeeEEEEeeeEEeeeerrrrrrrrrrrrrrrrereeeeereeeeeeeeeee” for the rest of your life.

Is this a good trade? I think not.

Young people who are certain, dead certain their future self will be OK with trading permanent ringing in their ears – audible over even lots of medium volume sounds, like the sound of rain – for “memories” and a handful of great short lived experiences, are wrong. There is some kind of saying: You can’t convince a stupid person that they are stupid, because they are stupid. (I bet Ed Latimore said it.) There is seriously viable math to describe how dumb it is:

Let’s assign the fun of going out to a nightclub for 4 hours to be 1,000 units of pleasantness. We’ll disregard the complexity added by factoring in that sometimes going out to a nightclub sucks ass. We’ll also disregard the cost of being so drunk you feel sick, the cost of feeling guilty about neglecting all the better uses of your time, and the cost of recognizing that you spent too much time on hedonism last month and fell behind in your studies. Keep it simple. 2 visits to a nightclub a month, 12 months in a year, 1,000 Pleasantness Units per visit. 2 * 12 * 1,000 = 24,000. Cool.

Now let’s look into the unpleasant costs of hearing ringing in your ears for 40 years. We’ll suppose the reader dies around age 65, which is pretty normal. Suppose the unpleasant ringing is noticed once a month, ten times over the span of 20 minutes, for approximately 8 seconds each before fading back out of awareness for like half a minute. Let’s assign the unpleasantness an estimated 3 Unpleasantness Units per entrance into awareness. (Yes, just 3.)

3 * the 10 times its noticed * 1x/mo * 12 mo/year = 30 * 12 = 360 Unpleasantness Units per year.

360/year * 40 years = 14,400 units of unpleasantness.

Note that the previous two paragraphs are unedited. I chose small numbers on purpose. Now I’ll choose some bigger numbers...!

Suppose the unpleasant ringing has a cost of 90 Unpleasantness Units and is noticed 10 times per span, at the same 1x/month rate, for 40 years. 90 is 30x the previous value of 3.

14,400 * 30 = 432,000

Now, I can’t do this math for you, and indeed your numbers will look different for you. They are super subjective numbers. You might be someone who has extraordinary social skills and thus gets a huge kick out of exercising them at a nightclub.

However, you also ought to consider that life is considerably more pleasant when you’ve stacked up dozens of permanent (or well maintained non-permanent) changes. It’s easier to tolerate the natural ebbs and flows of life, the continuous and indifferent suffering of the universe, when Former You did Future You a lot of favors. The other side of the same coin: When you’ve built up a dozen injuries with permanent downsides, slacked on education, slacked on building WiFi Money, haven’t ate a great diet, haven’t gone to the gym regularly (ever), life can be perilous.

How do you make something good out of so many permanent downsides?

And hearing damage is a permanent downside.

Thus, what is the cost of the ringing? Is it just that you hear the ringing?

Or does the cost extend further, to the ringing being yet another reminder of all your past bad decisions?

Do you want to bare that cost for the rest of your life?

Sounds bad to me.

* * * *

I presumed that every reader of this article would be under the age of 35. That assumption has proved true so far, as I prepare to send it out to people.

I had one more scene come to me as I reviewed my above writing:

Consider that some day, in the distant future, you will likely have your own child. And as an infant, this child will rest in a crib. If you’ve evaded a ringing in your ears until then, you’ll be able to hear your child moving in his crib: The rubbing of his or her clothing against the cloth of the crib’s blankets might even be audible to you. So will his breathing. But only if you haven’t damaged your hearing too badly to hear those low decibel sounds.

I leave you with the thought that hearing loss doesn’t sound like silence or even quiet. It sounds like ringing; permanent ringing. The ringing is with you from the time you wake up, to the time you’re in the office (where mine is far beyond audible over the quiet of my laptop), to the time your head hits the pillow at night. And there, with your window open, if you’ve fucked up your ability to hear quiet sounds, all you will hear, is ringing.

So if you are young enough to have friends who still go to bars and nightclubs, I invite you to share this  document with them, and tell them the author hopes they change their minds about their weekend rituals.

Adieu,
